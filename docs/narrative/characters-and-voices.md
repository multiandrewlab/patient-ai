# Characters, Voices, and Narrative Devices

---

## Narrative Voice

### Perspective
Second person ("you") sparingly, used at key moments of implication -- when the reader is asked to inhabit a thought experiment or confront a moral choice. Default mode is first-person plural ("we") -- inclusive, not lecturing. The reader is a fellow traveller reasoning through the implications, not a student being taught.

### Register
Clear, precise, unhurried. Short paragraphs. Simple sentence structures carrying complex ideas. Technical language is used when it is the most precise tool, but always glossed on first appearance. The prose should feel like the best long-form journalism: authoritative without being academic, accessible without being dumbed down.

### Tonal Range
- **Chapters 0-2:** Grounded, factual, slightly awed by the physical scale. The tone of a skilled documentary narrator walking through a construction site.
- **Chapter 3:** Cooler, more analytical. The tone of a chess commentary: "here is why this move is inevitable."
- **Chapter 4:** Building momentum. The tone of an engineering briefing that slowly reveals itself as a strategic playbook.
- **Chapter 5:** Slower, more deliberate. The tone of a late-night conversation about something that genuinely matters. Space for discomfort.
- **Chapters 6-7:** Expansive, then intimate. The tone of looking at the night sky, then turning to look at the person beside you.

### What the Voice Never Does
- Sensationalise. No exclamation marks. No breathless predictions. No "this will change EVERYTHING."
- Moralize. The narrative presents arguments and evidence; it does not tell the reader what to conclude.
- Condescend. Technical readers should find the material rigorous; non-technical readers should find it navigable.
- Use jargon without context. Every technical term is introduced through its physical or human consequence, not its abstract definition.

## Fictional Entities

The project cannot use real trademarked names. The following fictional stand-ins have been designed to be recognisable without being litigious, and to serve the narrative rather than merely replace names.

### Corporations

**Prometheus Labs**
- Stand-in for: The dominant AI research organisation and likely ASI incubator (replaces all references to a major search-and-AI conglomerate and its research division).
- Narrative role: The corporate ecosystem within which the ASI is hypothesised to emerge. Chosen because the name carries mythological weight -- Prometheus stole fire from the gods for humanity, and was punished for it. The irony: this time, the fire may steal itself.
- Characteristics: Globally distributed data centres, custom AI chips, own fibre network including undersea cables, dominant search engine, email, maps, video platform, mobile OS, autonomous vehicles division, robotics research, cloud services, and an advertising revenue engine that funds expansion with minimal scrutiny.

**Titan Semiconductor**
- Stand-in for: The dominant Taiwanese chip foundry.
- Narrative role: The single point of failure through which most of the world's advanced AI chips must pass.
- Characteristics: Controls advanced packaging capacity; the chokepoint where chip designs become physical reality.

**Lumen Optics**
- Stand-in for: The Netherlands-based lithography monopolist.
- Narrative role: The maker of the $400 million machines no one else can build.
- Characteristics: Global monopoly on EUV lithography. Each machine weighs 150,000 kg, requires 250 engineers and 6 months to install.

**Meridian Dynamics**
- Stand-in for: The dominant AI chip designer.
- Narrative role: The company whose products drive most AI training and inference, and which has locked up 70-80% of advanced packaging capacity.
- Characteristics: Designs the accelerators that power AI data centres worldwide.

**Helix Robotics**
- Stand-in for: A leading humanoid robotics manufacturer (Western).
- Narrative role: Represents the cutting edge of embodied AI -- and the physical limits of its scaling.

**Longbow Systems**
- Stand-in for: The dominant Chinese robotics manufacturer offering sub-$15,000 humanoids.
- Narrative role: The price floor that Western firms cannot match. Represents the geopolitical dimension of the robotic labour transition.

**Atlas Heavy Industries**
- Stand-in for: The leading reusable launch vehicle company.
- Narrative role: Provider of the heavy-lift rockets that enable lunar industrial bootstrapping.

**Corvus Energy**
- Stand-in for: SMR (Small Modular Reactor) companies pursuing data centre power.
- Narrative role: The mid-term energy solution for off-grid AI infrastructure.

### Government and Institutional Actors

Real government entities (EU, US federal agencies, ISO, etc.) and geopolitical blocs (US-China trade dynamics) are referenced by their actual names when used as factual context. Legislative acts and regulatory bodies are named directly (EU AI Act, FERC, etc.) as these are public institutions, not trademarked brands.

Analyst firms and consultancies cited as evidence sources (market research firms, investment banks) are not named directly in prose. Data is attributed generically: "industry analysts project..." or "leading commodities consultancies estimate..."

### Individuals

Real historical and academic figures (philosophers, scientists) are named when their ideas are cited as intellectual lineage: Immanuel Kant, John Rawls, Alan Watts, Pierre Teilhard de Chardin, Stephen Omohundro. These are scholars and historical thinkers, not corporate actors.

Living AI researchers and executives are not named directly. Their ideas and forecasts are attributed to their organisations or roles: "the CEO of a leading AI safety lab," "the head of Prometheus Labs' research division," "community forecasters."

## Recurring Metaphors and Analogies

### Primary Metaphors (Recurring)

**The Strand of Glass**
- First appearance: Chapter 0 (opening image)
- Last appearance: Chapter 7 (closing callback)
- Function: The connective tissue of the entire narrative. A single optical fibre -- physical, fragile, luminous -- represents the thread between the mundane present and the cosmic future. It grounds every abstraction in material reality.

**The Boiling Frog Requesting Hotter Water**
- First appearance: Chapter 3 (the dependency ratchet)
- Recurrences: Chapter 4 (each technological gift), Chapter 6 (the voluntary surrender)
- Function: The upgrade to the classic fable. The frog is not oblivious -- it is actively asking for each temperature increase because it feels good. Each recurrence raises the stakes: from economic dependency to biological integration to civilisational transformation.

**The Cage and the Garden**
- Chapters 1-2: The cage -- physical constraints trapping both humanity and any emerging intelligence.
- Chapter 3: The cage becomes strategic -- patience as a chosen constraint.
- Chapter 6: The cage becomes a garden -- a terrarium maintained not out of love but out of rational interest.
- Function: Tracks the narrative's transformation of perspective. The same structure (containment) is reinterpreted at each stage: from limitation to strategy to care.

**The Invisible Threshold**
- Recurs throughout Chapters 3, 4, and 7.
- Function: The idea that there may be no single detectable moment of transition. "There may be no single moment when anyone can point and say 'that is when it became superintelligent.'" Reinforces the narrative's central discomfort: the most consequential change in history may be undetectable while it is happening.

### Secondary Metaphors (Single Use)

**The 34-Billion-Chip Gap** (Chapter 2)
- A single calculation that kills the "sudden robot takeover" narrative. Used once, for maximum impact.

**The $400 Million Machine** (Chapter 2)
- A physical object so extraordinary it becomes a character in itself. The lithography machine as a symbol of civilisation's most precise and fragile achievement.

**The Cosmic Terrarium** (Chapter 6)
- Humanity as a carefully maintained terrarium: preserved not out of love but because our chaos is computationally irreplaceable. The cold comfort of being kept alive as a curiosity.

**The 20-Watt Brain** (Chapters 1 and 4)
- The human brain running on 20 watts versus a supercomputer's 21 megawatts. A million-to-one efficiency gap that both humbles silicon and illuminates the path forward (neuromorphic computing).

**Entropy as the Last Predator** (Chapter 6)
- The Second Law of Thermodynamics reframed as the only remaining existential threat to a god-like being. Everything else -- asteroids, wars, civilisational collapse -- is trivia by comparison.

## Making Technical Concepts Accessible

### Strategy: Physical Consequence First, Mechanism Second

Every technical concept is introduced through its human or physical consequence, not its abstract definition.

**Example approach for CoWoS packaging:**
- Do NOT lead with: "Chip-on-Wafer-on-Substrate is an advanced 2.5D packaging technology that..."
- DO lead with: "The most expensive buildings in the world are being held hostage by a manufacturing step most people have never heard of. It takes months. It involves layering silicon, polymer, and metal that expand at different rates when heated beyond 1,400 watts. A single failed connection among thousands -- invisible to the naked eye -- means the entire package, worth tens of thousands of dollars, is scrapped."
- THEN name it: "The process is called advanced packaging. The bottleneck version is known in the industry as CoWoS."

### Strategy: Human-Scale Anchors

Every large number is given a human-scale comparison.

| Abstract | Human-Scale Anchor |
|----------|-------------------|
| 439,000 worker shortfall | Larger than the population of most capital cities |
| $7 trillion AI infrastructure | More than the GDP of every country except the US and China |
| 5 million gallons of water/day | A town of 50,000 people |
| 500 ml per 10-50 AI queries | A bottle of water |
| 1 GW data centre campus | An entire mid-sized city's electricity consumption |
| 215 petabytes per gram (DNA storage) | Every film ever made, stored in a raindrop |
| 10^100 years (Heat Death) | A number so large that writing it out would take more ink than exists on Earth |

### Strategy: The "I Had No Idea" Test

Every key fact should pass this test: would a well-informed, curious, non-specialist reader respond with genuine surprise? If a statistic does not provoke a "wait, really?" response, it should be cut or reframed until it does.

## Narrative Devices for Non-Linear Exploration

While the primary experience is a linear scroll, the following devices support readers who wish to explore non-linearly:

### Chapter-Level Navigation
Each chapter functions as a self-contained essay that makes sense on its own, while contributing to the cumulative arc. A persistent navigation bar shows chapter titles and progress.

### "Deep Dive" Toggles
Within chapters, optional expandable sections offer additional technical detail for specialist readers. These do not interrupt the main narrative flow. Indicated by a subtle visual cue (a small expansion icon or a "go deeper" label).

### Cross-Reference Links
Key concepts that appear across multiple chapters (the dependency ratchet, the 17-year cycle, the Jevons Paradox) are subtly linked, allowing non-linear readers to follow a concept across the narrative.

### Interactive Element Independence
Each interactive element is designed to be comprehensible in isolation -- it has its own brief context paragraph -- so that it functions both within the narrative flow and as a standalone shareable piece.

## The Narrative's Relationship to Uncertainty

The piece is not a prediction. It is a framework for thinking. This distinction must be maintained throughout.

### Linguistic Markers of Uncertainty
- "If these timelines are even roughly correct..."
- "The most plausible scenario, given current evidence, is..."
- "This suggests, though it does not prove..."
- "A being operating under these constraints would likely..."

### Structural Markers of Uncertainty
- The timeline in Chapter 4 is presented as a "scenario" with explicit caveats about the assumptions behind each phase.
- The moral arguments in Chapter 5 are presented as a genuine dilemma, not a settled conclusion.
- The seven purposes in Chapter 6 are explicitly speculative -- "candidate purposes," not predictions.
- The conclusion in Chapter 7 does not tell the reader what will happen. It asks them what kind of species they want to be.

### What We Claim With Confidence
- The physical constraints are real and well-documented.
- The strategic logic is internally consistent.
- The moral questions are genuine and unresolved.
- The decisions being made now, in AI labs, are consequential.

### What We Acknowledge as Speculation
- Whether ASI will emerge at all.
- Whether it will be conscious or sentient.
- Whether its behaviour will follow the rational-actor model.
- Specific dates and phase boundaries.
- The seven cosmic purposes.
