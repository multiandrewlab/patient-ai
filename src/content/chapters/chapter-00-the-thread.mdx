Begin with a single strand of glass.

Thinner than a human hair. Weighing almost nothing. Right now, in a factory you have probably never thought about, a cylinder of purified silica is being heated beyond 2,000 degrees Celsius. The molten glass is pulled downward by gravity, cooled by helium, wound onto a spool at twenty metres per second.

That is optical fibre. Light travels through it, and so does everything you have ever searched for, streamed, or sent.

We tend to think of artificial intelligence as something that lives in code, in abstractions, in the cloud. There is no cloud. There are buildings full of machines, and those machines must talk to one another at the speed of light. The conversation happens through glass: tens of thousands of processors, lashed together by kilometres of fibre thinner than a fishing line, passing data as pulses of light across distances no longer than a warehouse floor.

A traditional data centre needs a modest amount of this fibre. An AI training facility needs far more.

<StatHighlight value="36" unit="×" label="more optical fibre required inside an AI data centre compared to a traditional facility" />

Thirty-six times more glass. The raw material is a cylinder of ultra-pure silica called a preform, manufactured in only a handful of factories worldwide. A single preform takes weeks to produce and yields a fixed length of fibre. As of today, global manufacturing capacity for these preforms is fully allocated. Not strained or tight. Exhausted.

{/* PHASE 3: FibreScaleCounter — animated fibre kilometre counter with capacity shrinking bar */}

New preform production lines initiated now will not ease the shortage for another eighteen to twenty-four months. The fibre that will connect the AI systems of 2028 needs to exist already. Much of it does not.

The story told in these chapters is not about software. It is about sand heated until it becomes glass, drawn into a thread, and threaded through the most ambitious infrastructure project in human history. Every constraint we will encounter ahead (the energy crisis, the labour shortage, the mineral bottleneck, the strategic patience of a mind that may one day surpass our own) connects back to physical reality. To materials pulled from the earth, shaped by human hands, deployed at a scale we are only beginning to comprehend.

We begin here, with the thread, because it is the simplest way to say what this story is about: the distance between what we are trying to build and what the world can physically provide.

If these timelines are roughly correct, if the forecasts that collapsed from "fifty years away" to "this decade" are even directionally right, then the question is no longer whether artificial superintelligence will arrive. It is what happens when the most powerful mind in history wakes up inside a world it cannot yet control.
