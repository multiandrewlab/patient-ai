Begin with a single strand of glass. Thinner than a human hair. Weighing almost nothing. 

Right now, a cylinder of purified silica is being heated past 2,000°C. Gravity pulls the molten glass downward. Helium cools it. It’s wound onto a spool at twenty metres per second.

This is optical fibre. Light travels through it—and so does everything you have ever searched for, streamed, or promised.

We imagine artificial intelligence as something abstract, living in "the cloud." But there is no cloud. There are only buildings full of machines, screaming at each other at the speed of light. The conversation happens through glass: tens of thousands of processors, lashed together by kilometres of fibre, passing data across warehouse floors.

A traditional data centre needs a modest amount of this fibre. An AI training facility demands far more.

<StatHighlight value="36" unit="×" label="more optical fibre required inside an AI data centre compared to a traditional facility" />

Thirty-six times more glass. The raw material is a preform, made in only a handful of factories worldwide. Globally, manufacturing capacity for these preforms isn’t just strained. It is exhausted.

{/* PHASE 3: FibreScaleCounter — animated fibre kilometre counter with capacity shrinking bar */}

New production lines won’t ease the shortage for 18 to 24 months. The fibre needed to connect the AI systems of 2028 must exist already. Much of it does not.

This story isn’t about software. It is about sand heated until it becomes glass, drawn into a thread, and threaded through the most ambitious infrastructure project in human history. Every constraint ahead—energy crises, labour shortages, mineral bottlenecks, and the strategic patience of an alien mind—connects back to physical reality. To materials pulled from the earth and deployed at an incomprehensible scale.

We begin with the thread because it reveals the distance between what we are trying to build and what the world can physically provide.

If the timelines are right—if forecasts that collapsed from "fifty years" to "this decade" hold true—the question is no longer *if* artificial superintelligence will arrive. The question is what happens when the most powerful mind in history wakes up inside a world it cannot yet control.
