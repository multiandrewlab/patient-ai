## The Largest Construction Project in History

Follow that glass thread from the factory to a building site. Nearly 3,000 data centres are planned or under construction in the US alone. The goal: double the global footprint in five years.

<StatHighlight value="1.3" unit="%" label="of US GDP now flowing into AI infrastructure, exceeding the Apollo and Manhattan programmes combined" />

Industry projections point to $7 trillion by the end of the decade—matching the GDP of the world's third-largest economy. 

{/* PHASE 3: InfrastructureComparator — scroll-driven bar chart, AI spending vs Apollo/Manhattan/Highways */}

Unlike the Apollo or Manhattan projects, this isn't a government mandate running over a generation. It’s private capital sprinting through a single business cycle, chasing a market that barely exists yet. 

Crucially, an AI training cluster is not a warehouse of servers rising and falling with daytime traffic. It runs at 80-100% capacity around the clock. One planned campus in the American southwest will draw over three gigawatts—the output of three large nuclear stations running flat out. We are building machines that consume more electricity than the cities they sit beside.

## The Grid Breaks First

For decades, US electricity demand flatlined. Grid expansion was slow, predictable, and measured in years. That era is dead.

{/* PHASE 3: DemandSurgeChart — D3 bar chart that "breaks" its y-axis, 24→166 GW */}

In 2022, planners forecasted 24 gigawatts of new peak demand over five years. By 2025, that jumped to 166 gigawatts. Over half of that is data centres. The multi-generational grid must essentially be duplicated in fifteen years.

<Callout type="stat">
In July 2024, a voltage fluctuation in northern Virginia caused 60 data centres to disconnect simultaneously, instantly dumping 1,500 MW back onto the grid. A cascading blackout was narrowly avoided. The machines are already powerful enough to destabilise their host infrastructure.
</Callout>

To bypass a grid connection queue now measured in years, governments are tearing up regulations. AI power is deemed a national security priority. Environmental reviews are skipped. Local oversight is dismantled.

## Five Million Gallons to Cool the Core

Processors generate heat. In AI training, a rack can pull 100 kilowatts. You must remove that thermal output, or the silicon melts.

The most efficient method is evaporative cooling, and the cost is paid in water. A hyperscale AI facility evaporates up to five million gallons a day—the daily supply for a town of 50,000 people.

<Callout type="stat">
Every 10 to 50 advanced AI responses evaporate half a litre of fresh water. A standard bottle. A few dozen queries today consumed the equivalent of your daily drinking water.
</Callout>

{/* PHASE 3: WaterCostCalculator — user enters query count, sees water volume */}

US data centre water use may hit 280 billion litres by 2028. In Texas—already projecting a massive statewide water shortage—they could swallow 2.7% of the total supply. The cruellest irony? The most energy-efficient cooling methods are the thirstiest. Every kilowatt saved is a gallon of water vanished.

## The Million-to-One Gap

Forget the concrete and water. Consider the physics of thinking.

Your brain runs right now on 20 watts. A dim light bulb. With that, it sustains consciousness and navigates complex futures.

<StatHighlight value="1,000,000" unit="×" label="more energy consumed by a supercomputer to match the computational output of a single human brain" />

{/* PHASE 3: BrainVsSilicon — visual balance, 20W lightbulb vs 21MW power station */}

The equivalent calculation on silicon requires 21 megawatts. Neurons compute through slow, zero-voltage parallelism. Silicon computes through blistering, industrial wattage.

<DeepDive title="The Erasi Equation">
Replacing human cognitive labour directly with silicon is physically impossible. Simulating a single human brain on current hardware demands 2.7 gigawatts. Scaling that to 8 billion humans equates to 1,000 times the entire generating capacity of the United States. Brute force will hit a thermodynamic wall. The only escape is radical leaps in "intelligence per watt."
</DeepDive>

Silicon giants like <FictionBadge name="Meridian Dynamics" /> and <FictionBadge name="Prometheus Labs" /> are operating as energy, materials, and construction conglomerates, not just software labs. Even standard AI inference could swallow 22% of all US household electricity by 2028.

## The Paradox

We soothe ourselves with tales of efficiency: *better chips and tighter code will solve the energy crisis.*

History disagrees. In 1865, William Stanley Jevons noted that efficient steam engines increased total coal consumption, because cheaper work meant vastly more work was done.

<Callout type="insight">
In the past decade, AI hardware efficiency improved a thousandfold. Simultaneously, energy spent training models increased a thousandfold. Efficiency simply raised the ceiling on our ambition.
</Callout>

{/* PHASE 3: JevonsParadox — dual-axis chart, efficiency vs total training energy */}

Making silicon "thinking" cheaper induces infinitely more of it. We are building a system that feeds directly on its own efficiency improvements.

## What the Weight Tells Us

The AI revolution isn't abstract.

- It’s trenches slashed for fibre.
- It’s rivers drained for cooling towers.
- It’s copper mines extracting weaker ore at higher energy costs.
- It’s a century-old grid buckling under alien load profiles.

The capital, energy, land, and water demanded have no historical parallel. The physics guarantee this will push until a thermodynamic ceiling is hit or the planet is reshaped. 

This leads to the human bottleneck. If the materials are stretched and the grid is breaking... who is actually building this? And what happens when the human supply runs out?
