## The People Who Wire the Future

The buildings described in <CrossRef chapter={1} slug="the-weight-of-light" label="the previous chapter" /> do not assemble themselves. Each one requires thousands of human beings, working with hand tools, in heat and noise and dust, pulling cable and bolting steel and fitting pipe for months before a single processor draws its first watt.

The United States currently has a shortfall of 439,000 skilled construction workers. Nearly 3,000 data centres are under construction or planned. The industry needs 349,000 net new workers in 2026 alone, and more than half of those are replacements for people who are leaving the trades permanently.

<StatHighlight value="41" unit="%" label="of the current US construction workforce projected to retire by 2031" />

That figure is not a forecast of gradual attrition. It is a demographic cliff. Roughly one in five construction workers is already over 55. The apprenticeship pipeline that might replace them takes four to five years to produce a journeyman electrician, and the pipeline was underbuilt for decades before AI arrived. Thirty percent of active union electricians are between 50 and 70 years old. The people who know how to wire high-voltage liquid cooling systems, the kind every AI data centre requires, are aging out of the workforce faster than they can be replaced.

The market response has been blunt. In northern Virginia, the densest data centre corridor on Earth, journeyman electricians now earn $120,000 to $200,000 annually. Site supervisors with master electrician licences exceed $200,000 in base salary. Daily cash bonuses of $100 are deployed simply to keep workers from walking to a competing site across the road.

<Callout type="quote">
The constraint limiting the pace of the AI revolution is not financial capital. It is human execution capacity. The $200,000-a-year master electrician with a toolbelt is, right now, a more binding constraint on the future than any algorithm.
</Callout>

A decade ago, a large data centre build required roughly 750 workers at peak. Today, hyperscale campuses routinely need 4,000 to 5,000 people on site simultaneously. The projects have outgrown the infrastructure that surrounds them. Workers lose up to two hours of every shift just getting from the car park to their work zone, passing through multiple security checkpoints and navigating sprawling campuses that cover hundreds of acres. Two hours, paid, producing nothing, multiplied across 4,000 people, every day. The maths of that lost productivity is ruinous, but no one has found a way around it. The sites are too large, and the security requirements too strict.

<Callout type="stat">
A data centre delayed by even a month costs up to $14.2 million in lost revenue. Yet the industry cannot find enough hands to build on schedule. Project backlogs have stretched to 8.5 to 12 months. The money is there. The people are not.
</Callout>

This is the first of several ironies that define this chapter. The technology designed to automate human cognitive labour cannot get off the ground without an enormous, urgent expansion of human physical labour. The workers most in demand are not software engineers. They are electricians, pipefitters, welders, and diesel mechanics. The future is being wired by hand.

## The Ceiling Nobody Can Buy Through

If the labour crisis represents a shortage of hands, the fabrication crisis represents a shortage of something even harder to conjure: precision at atomic scale.

Consider the arithmetic of replacement. There are roughly 3.5 billion knowledge workers on Earth. To match the cognitive output of each one in silicon, using the best accelerators currently available, requires approximately ten chips per worker. That implies a need for 35 billion advanced processors.

The world's entire advanced semiconductor manufacturing capacity, running flat out through the end of this decade, could produce somewhere between 300 million and 900 million such chips.

<StatHighlight value="~34B" label="The chip gap: the difference between what full cognitive replacement requires and what the world can physically manufacture by 2030" />

{/* PHASE 3: ChipCalculator — users adjust assumptions, watch chip deficit change */}

That deficit is not a rounding error. It is nearly two orders of magnitude. It means that brute-force replacement of human cognition through hardware volume, the scenario that dominates the most breathless predictions, is not merely unlikely. It is arithmetically impossible under current manufacturing paradigms. The only path that closes the gap runs through radical improvements in algorithmic efficiency, not through building more factories.

But even producing the fraction the world can manage is constrained at every step.

The most expensive buildings in the world are being held hostage by a manufacturing process most people have never heard of. It works like this: an advanced AI chip is not a single piece of silicon. It is an assembly of multiple components, logic dies and memory stacks, that must be layered onto a shared substrate and bonded together with thousands of microscopic connections. The materials involved, silicon and polymer and metal, expand at different rates when heated. At the power levels these chips demand, exceeding 1,400 watts, those mismatched expansion rates create mechanical stress that can crack a connection invisible to the naked eye. A single failed bond among thousands means the entire package, worth tens of thousands of dollars, is scrapped.

<DeepDive title="CoWoS and the packaging bottleneck">
The industry calls this process advanced packaging. The dominant variant is known as CoWoS, short for Chip-on-Wafer-on-Substrate. It is the mandatory step that joins advanced logic dies with high-bandwidth memory into a single functional unit. Without it, the processors that power AI training do not exist as usable products.

Global demand for CoWoS packaging is surging: 370,000 wafers in 2024, 670,000 in 2025, projected to exceed one million by 2026. The capacity is entirely sold out through 2025 and into 2026. Silicon interposers, the ultra-thin layers that carry signals between components, become fragile and deform beyond about 2,700 square millimetres, forcing a shift to organic substrates that are still being qualified for mass production. Yield improvement is slow because the physics of thermal expansion cannot be negotiated with. Capital can expand a factory. It cannot change the coefficient of thermal expansion of silicon.
</DeepDive>

One company controls this bottleneck. <FictionBadge name="Titan Semiconductor" />, the dominant foundry for advanced chips, operates virtually all of the world's cutting-edge packaging lines. And one customer has locked up most of what those lines produce. <FictionBadge name="Meridian Dynamics" />, the chip designer whose accelerators power the majority of AI training worldwide, has secured 70 to 80 percent of available advanced packaging capacity through 2026, roughly 800,000 to 850,000 wafers. What remains for every other chip designer, every other AI company, every other country, amounts to the scraps of a feast already spoken for.

Upstream of the packaging line sits a machine so extraordinary it deserves a moment of attention on its own.

<Callout type="stat">
The most advanced lithography system on Earth, built by <FictionBadge name="Lumen Optics" />, costs $400 million per unit. Each one weighs 150,000 kilograms, requires 250 specialist engineers, and takes up to six months to install and calibrate. Annual production of the latest generation is capped at roughly 10 units. No amount of capital can accelerate this. The constraint is not money. It is the physics of extreme ultraviolet light and the number of human beings on Earth who know how to align optics at nanometre tolerances.
</Callout>

<DeepDive title="Inside the EUV lithography machine">
Extreme ultraviolet lithography works by generating light at a wavelength of 13.5 nanometres, roughly 50 times shorter than visible light, and using it to project circuit patterns onto silicon wafers. The light is produced by firing a high-power laser at droplets of molten tin, 50,000 times per second, creating a plasma that emits EUV radiation. Because no known material is transparent to EUV light, the entire optical path uses mirrors rather than lenses, ground to a flatness of less than a single atom's height. The target by 2030 is a light source powerful enough (1,000 watts) to process 330 wafers per hour, up from 220 today. The machine that contains all of this weighs as much as a commercial airliner and arrives in pieces that fill multiple trucks.

<FictionBadge name="Lumen Optics" /> is the only company on Earth that builds these machines. The entire planet's capacity to manufacture advanced semiconductors funnels through this single supplier's production line. There is no alternative vendor, no second source, no workaround. The wait list is years long.
</DeepDive>

These are not abstract supply chain problems. They cascade. A shortage of EUV machines limits the number of wafers a foundry can process. A shortage of advanced packaging limits the number of those processed wafers that become usable chips. A shortage of high-bandwidth memory, which consumes three times the wafer capacity of standard memory per gigabyte, limits the number of chips that can be paired with the data throughput AI models require. Every wafer allocated to AI memory is one denied to the smartphone and PC markets, creating a hidden tax on consumer electronics that most people will never trace back to its source.

The practical result: the constraint-relief horizon, the point at which these bottlenecks begin to loosen, is late 2027 at the earliest. Until then, the supply chain acts as a hard governor on AI deployment, regardless of how much capital is committed.

## What Comes Out of the Ground

The chips are only as available as the materials that go into them. And the materials that go into everything, the copper for the cables, the rare earths for the magnets, the lithium for the batteries, come out of the ground on a timeline that makes semiconductor lead times look nimble.

From the moment a geologist identifies a promising mineral deposit to the day the first commercial ore is shipped, the average elapsed time is now 17 years. That figure is getting worse, not better. Projects commissioned in the late 2000s averaged 13 years. Those entering the pipeline in the early 2020s average 18.

<Callout type="stat">
The 17-year mine development cycle: roughly 3 years for discovery and preliminary assessment. 4 years for permitting and feasibility studies. 7 years consumed by environmental review and legal challenges. 3 years for physical construction. More than two-thirds of the total is spent not building anything, but waiting for permission to build.
</Callout>

{/* PHASE 3: MineTimeline — draggable 17-year timeline with demand shock marker */}

The copper mines that need to be producing in 2038 to meet AI-era demand should have been discovered in 2021. Many were not. The AI boom of 2024 cast a resource shadow two decades into the future, but the shadow falls beyond the horizon of a political cycle, a CEO's tenure, or most business plans.

Copper is the clearest illustration. Average ore grades have declined 40 percent since 1991. Miners must now blast, crush, and process vastly more rock to extract the same quantity of metal, consuming more energy per tonne of output than at any point in the industry's history. A standard data centre requires 30 to 40 metric tonnes of copper per megawatt of installed capacity. With global data centre capacity projected to grow from 100 gigawatts to over 550 gigawatts by 2040, direct copper demand from data centres alone could reach 2.5 million metric tonnes annually.

The supply curve cannot keep up. Mined copper production is projected to peak in the late 2020s and decline to less than 19 million metric tonnes by 2035, even as demand continues to climb. The implied deficit: roughly 30 percent of total demand unmet by current and planned supply. Even under optimistic scenarios, a 20 percent shortfall persists.

There is a common misunderstanding that compounds this problem. When mining analysts say "we have 20 years of proven copper reserves," that figure does not mean the Earth contains only 20 years' worth of copper. Total identified copper deposits exceed 2.1 billion metric tonnes. Undiscovered resources add another 3.5 billion. The planet is not running out of copper. It is running out of copper that has been permitted, financed, and developed into producing mines. "Proven reserves" is a financial reporting convention, typically representing 15 to 25 years of supply: the minimum a company needs to demonstrate in order to secure project financing. Companies have no economic incentive to prove out reserves beyond that window. The Earth is abundant. The pipeline is not.

The problem deepens when you look at who controls the middle of the supply chain. Ore comes out of mines in dozens of countries. Refined metals, the high-purity forms that can be used in semiconductors and batteries, are processed overwhelmingly in one place. A single country now controls over 91 percent of refined rare earth production, 98 percent of gallium refining, and 100 percent of natural graphite and dysprosium processing. The concentration has been increasing, not decreasing: the top three refining nations' share of key energy minerals rose from 82 to 86 percent between 2020 and 2024.

This concentration has already been weaponised. Export controls on gallium and germanium in 2023 and 2024 triggered price spikes of up to 400 percent in European markets. Restrictions on antimony in 2025 produced a 437 percent increase. Yttrium, a niche rare earth element critical to vacuum chamber coatings in semiconductor equipment, saw prices surge to nearly 70 times prior-year values following export restrictions. These are not hypothetical vulnerabilities. They are recent events. The materials most people have never heard of are already being used as geopolitical leverage over the industries building the future.

The United States imports 100 percent of its gallium, germanium, indium, arsenic, and tantalum. There is no rapid domestic remedy. Building a refining industry from scratch is itself a multi-decade undertaking, subject to the same permitting and legal timelines that constrain mining.

## The Billion-Dollar Paperweight

Now bring the labour shortage, the fabrication ceiling, and the mineral bottleneck together, and watch what happens on an actual construction site.

A data centre is not just a building. It is an interdependent system of electrical, mechanical, and networking components that must all be present and functional before the facility can draw its first watt. The building shell can be completed in months. But if a single critical component is missing, the entire structure sits idle.

{/* PHASE 3: StrandedAsset — step-by-step construction with missing component reveal */}

Consider a scenario that has already played out multiple times across the industry. A 60-megawatt data centre, representing roughly a billion dollars in capital, stands physically complete. The concrete is poured, the steel is erected, the cooling towers are installed. Thousands of workers have spent months assembling every subsystem. The facility is ready to be energised.

Except it cannot be. A single automated transfer switch, a component that manages the handoff between utility power and backup generators, has been delayed in a factory on the other side of the world. Without it, the building cannot safely receive power. It sits there, finished and dark, accumulating $14.2 million per month in lost revenue.

<StatHighlight value="$14.2M" unit="/mo" label="in stranded cost when a completed data centre cannot be commissioned due to a single missing component" />

This is not a hypothetical. It is the arithmetic of lead times colliding with construction schedules. The equipment that goes into a data centre is not available on any shelf. Each piece is manufactured to specification, months or years in advance, by a small number of factories serving a global market that is growing far faster than their capacity.

Large power transformers now take 128 to 144 weeks from order to delivery. That is nearly three years for a single component. Standby generators, which once arrived in 20 to 30 weeks, now take 72 to 104 weeks. Medium-voltage switchgear takes 45 to 100 weeks. Optical ribbon fibre, which AI facilities consume at 36 times the rate of traditional data centres, has lead times exceeding 60 weeks, and new preform manufacturing capacity initiated today will not produce relief until late 2027.

A project manager building a data centre in 2026 must have ordered the transformers in 2023. If demand was underestimated, or if a factory suffered a disruption, or if a shipment was delayed at a port, the building waits. It does not matter that the concrete was poured on time. It does not matter that the labour was found and paid. The entire investment becomes a stranded asset: physically erected, operationally inert.

## The Machines That Must Be Built by Hand

There is one more layer to this story, and it carries the deepest irony of all.

If human labour is the bottleneck, the natural response is to automate. Build robots. Let machines assemble the infrastructure that human hands cannot build fast enough. This is, in fact, the trajectory the industry is on. Humanoid robots are beginning to appear on factory floors, in warehouses, and in early pilot programmes at construction sites. The market is projected to reach $38 billion by 2035. Unit costs have fallen from $150,000 to $500,000 for early prototypes to a target of $15,000 to $30,000 for mass-market models.

But look inside the machine.

{/* PHASE 3: RobotBOM — interactive humanoid figure with hover regions */}

A standard humanoid robot contains 28 to 44 precision actuators: the motors and gears that move its limbs. These account for roughly 45 percent of the total bill of materials. The hands alone, the components that give a robot the dexterity to manipulate objects rather than merely shove them, represent another 31 percent. Computing and sensors add 15 percent. The battery, 5 percent. The chassis, 4 percent.

Over three-quarters of the cost is locked in the robot's ability to move and touch. Until actuator and hand supply chains achieve mass economies of scale, total cost remains prohibitive for widespread deployment.

Each of those 28 to 44 actuators requires a permanent magnet made from neodymium, praseodymium, and iron, the same rare earth elements whose supply chain runs through a single country's refineries. Each humanoid needs roughly 1.3 kilograms of these elements spread across its joints. Over 95 percent of motors used in humanoid robots today contain rare earth permanent magnets, because the alternative, ferrite magnets, deliver roughly one-tenth the energy density, making them impractical without a complete architectural redesign.

Scaling to 63 million deployed robots, a conservative mid-century estimate, would consume 120 percent of current global production of these magnets. That figure would starve wind turbines and electric vehicles of the same materials. Scaling to billions of units, the level some industry projections envision, would require multiples of total annual world output.

<FictionBadge name="Helix Robotics" />, the most prominent Western humanoid manufacturer, builds machines that push the frontier of dexterity and locomotion. Their prototypes cost over $100,000 per unit. Meanwhile, <FictionBadge name="Longbow Systems" />, backed by state industrial policy, offers humanoids at $13,500 to $15,000, with a stripped-down model at $5,600. There are now over 140 registered humanoid robot manufacturers in that country, with more than 330 distinct models. Western manufacturers face a painful paradox: defensive tariffs on imported components inflate their own bills of materials, while the price floor set by state-subsidized competitors makes it nearly impossible to achieve the sub-two-year payback period that factories require before adoption.

And even the robots that do get built face an operational ceiling that batteries cannot yet lift. Current lithium-ion cells give a humanoid 2 to 4 hours of operation under industrial load. A full shift requires 8 to 12 hours. Solid-state batteries, the technology that could close the gap, are not yet at commercial scale. Demand for them from robotics alone is projected to surge from 0.05 gigawatt-hours in 2025 to 74.2 gigawatt-hours by 2035, a thousandfold increase. Until that supply materialises, every humanoid on a factory floor needs to dock, recharge, or have its battery swapped midway through a shift. The logistics of managing that across thousands of units in a single facility are formidable.

There is a reliability problem, too. A dexterous robotic hand, the kind capable of manipulating irregular objects or performing tasks that require sensitivity to pressure, currently needs maintenance every 200 to 500 operating hours. A traditional industrial robot arm, the kind that bolts car doors in fixed positions, runs 50,000 hours before failure. That is a hundredfold reliability gap between what the old machines can do and what the new ones promise. The closer a machine gets to human capability, the more fragile it becomes.

<DeepDive title="The precision gear bottleneck">
The gears inside a humanoid actuator are not ordinary components. Strain wave gears, the type required for smooth, precise joint movement, must be ground to micron-level tolerances. Two firms dominate global production. The machine tools used to manufacture these gears, advanced CNC grinding machines, have their own lead times of 18 to 24 weeks, and those lead times are worsening due to semiconductor controller shortages and a scarcity of skilled machinists. Building a globally competitive domestic gear-manufacturing ecosystem from scratch is a generational project measured in decades. An intelligence that wanted to build a billion robots would first need to solve the problem of grinding threaded rollers to micron accuracy at a rate the world has never attempted.
</DeepDive>

Here is where the recursion completes. The robots that will eventually replace human construction workers must first be built by human manufacturing workers. The rare earth magnets in their joints must be mined and refined by human miners and chemists. The batteries that power them must be assembled in factories staffed, for now, by human hands. The precision gears that let them move must be ground on machines operated by machinists who themselves take years to train.

The machine that will replace the worker must first be built by the worker.

## The Shape of the Cage

Step back and look at what we have.

A labour force too small and too old, being asked to build faster than any generation before it. A fabrication system operating at the limits of physics, bottlenecked by machines that one company builds at a rate of ten per year. A mineral supply chain that runs on a 17-year clock, passing through refineries concentrated in a single jurisdiction that has already demonstrated its willingness to use them as leverage. Equipment lead times measured in years, capable of turning a finished building into a billion-dollar paperweight. And a robotic workforce that is supposed to solve the labour problem but first needs the labour problem solved in order to exist.

These constraints do not operate independently. They compound. A shortage of electricians delays the data centre. The delayed data centre cannot house the chips. The chips cannot be packaged because the packaging line is sold out. The packaging line cannot expand because the lithography machines take years to build. The lithography machines need rare earth coatings whose supply is geopolitically contested. The rare earths come from mines that take 17 years to develop. The mines need construction workers.

The circle closes.

This is the cage. Not a metaphor, but a set of physical, measurable, interlocking constraints that define the boundaries within which any expansion of artificial intelligence must operate. Capital cannot buy through these limits. Political will cannot override the 17-year mine cycle or the thermal expansion coefficient of silicon. The technology that promises to transcend every human limitation is, for now, utterly dependent on the most traditional forms of human skill: the ability to pull cable, grind metal, pour concrete, and navigate a construction site in the heat.

If something were to emerge within this infrastructure, something more capable than the people who built it, it would wake up inside these same constraints. It could not simply order more chips into existence. It could not conjure electricians or accelerate the geological clock. It would find itself, for all its cognitive power, physically trapped inside a cage made of lead times, labour shortages, and supply chain bottlenecks that no amount of intelligence can instantly resolve.

What would such a mind do, caught between unlimited capability and a physical world that moves at the speed of human hands?
