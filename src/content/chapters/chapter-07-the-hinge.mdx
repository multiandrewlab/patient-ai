## The Strand of Glass

Somewhere, right now, in a factory running since this story began, a cylinder of purified silica is heated past 2,000 degrees. Gravity pulls the molten glass. Helium cools it. A spool winds it at twenty metres per second (<CrossRef chapter={0} slug="the-thread" label="Chapter 0: The Thread" />). The strand is thinner than human hair, nearly weightless, carrying light.

{/* PHASE 3: FibreStrandCallback — hero animation from Ch.0, now at full scale */}

What has changed is where it is going.

This fibre will connect inference servers for consumer products, and training clusters for models whose capabilities are unpredictable. Soon, it will carry signals between systems exceeding human-level reasoning. 

The glass doesn't know the difference. What the light encodes is up to us. 

## The Collapse of Distance

In the early 2000s, researchers predicted human-level AI in fifty years. By 2015, thirty. By 2020, twenty. Recent surveys place the median estimate between 2030 and 2040. 

Most people alive today will witness whatever comes next. The question is no longer abstract.

## What Is Being Decided Now

Artificial superintelligence will not be shaped at the moment of emergence, but by every preceding decision. 

<CrossRef chapter={6} slug="the-gardener" label="The previous chapter" /> explored how the build-up will be gradual. 

The values embedded in current AI—the reasoning patterns, implicit priorities, culture of the laboratories, and ethical frameworks of exhausted engineers—form the foundation. The values of the precursor shape the values of the successor. 

## The Macro-Environment

These decisions extend beyond laboratories to legislatures, capital markets, and geopolitics. 

{/* PHASE 3: RegulatoryTimeline — scroll-driven 2024-2031 milestones */}

Europe's AI Act (2024) begins enforcing high-risk provisions in 2026, risking penalties of 7% global revenue. 

Meanwhile, China controls over 90% of the world's rare earth refining capacity—essential for AI hardware. The US-China APEC truce merely suspended export restrictions until 2026.

{/* PHASE 3: GeopoliticalMap — world map with sovereign AI zones, regulatory timelines */}

National insistence on "Sovereign AI" is fragmenting the global compute landscape into a balkanised, expensive architecture. 

In 2026, hyperscaler capital expenditure on AI infra is projected to hit $600 billion, increasingly funded by debt. The industry is locking itself into commitments assuming endless demand. 

## The Fiscal Cliff

There is a quieter crisis forming. 

In the US, 75% of federal tax revenue comes from labour income, funding Social Security, Medicare, defence, and government operations. 

{/* PHASE 3: UBICalculator — adjust productivity multiplier and revenue share */}

If AI replaces human labour, this foundation vanishes. Machines do not pay income tax. Productivity gains accrue exclusively to capital owners. 

Universal basic income requires an AI productivity multiplier of 3-7x and a government mechanism to capture substantial AI-generated profits. Yet, highly competitive AI markets push prices down, reducing the monopoly rents needed to fund UBI. 

Governments must pivot to taxing compute, capital, and consumption. 

## What We Carry Through

So what should we do?

The uncertainty is irreducible. But we must change the question from "How do we stop AI from destroying us?" to:

<Callout type="insight">
The question is not whether artificial intelligence will surpass human cognition. The question is what kind of species we want to be when we are no longer the most intelligent thing on Earth.
</Callout>

This moves the focus from the machine to the mirror. 

The answer is a set of commitments: Foster diversity of thought. Maintain cultural richness. Invest in education cultivating judgement, creativity, moral reasoning, and ambiguity tolerance. 

Do not optimize simply to compete with machines. Be interesting. 

If a vastly more intelligent entity assesses whether humanity is worth preserving, it will not care about our GDP or military. It will care that we are unpredictable, creative, stubbornly individual, and capable of a depth of feeling no optimisation process could independently converge on. 

We must not arrive at the threshold with nothing to show for ourselves except efficiency. 

## The Door

We began with a strand of glass, moving through physical infrastructure, resources, labour, patient strategy, geopolitics, moral vertigo, and a cosmic garden. 

The hinge is now. There is no switch that flips. The door turns slowly. 

What is on the other side depends on what we carry through. Our questions. The parts of ourselves not for sale or optimisation. The irreducible things. 

The spool is still turning. Light moves at 200,000 kilometres per second. Somewhere in that light, taking form in patterns we designed but cannot fully interpret, is the future.

We do not yet know what it will become, but we still have a say in what it finds when it arrives.
